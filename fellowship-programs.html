The standard

Principles that hold the work

These tests connect public purpose to responsible practice.

01

Field alignment

A Fellow's knowledge and capabilities should be relevant to the work the appointment requires. In this context, consider humanity, civic, and education.

02

Institutional fit

The host setting should make the contribution feasible, responsible, and useful. In this context, consider science, earth, and innovation.

03

Public value

The appointment should create benefit beyond private career advancement or organizational convenience. In this context, consider graduate and national capacity.

From intent to practice

From interest to appointment

Appointments are shaped deliberately so that recognition, institutional affiliation, and useful work reinforce one another.

Express interest

Describe your record, field, mission interests, and the contribution you are ready to make. Here, attend to humanity, civic, and education: Service involving dignity, public trust, community strength, learning, mentorship, and opportunity.

Establish fit

Consider merit, readiness, mission alignment, and the setting in which the work can succeed. Here, attend to science, earth, and innovation: Research, discovery, stewardship, public-interest technology, and responsible solution-building.

Define the appointment

Confirm scope, host support, responsibilities, intended benefit, and a practical working cadence. Here, attend to graduate and national capacity: Field-aligned appointments and expertise applied to resilient institutions and critical public systems.

Serve and reflect

Complete the work with rigor, participate in Society life, and share transferable learning. Here, attend to senior and distinguished: Experienced leaders whose contribution may include advising, convening, mentoring, and major missions.

Put this work into practice

Begin with humanity, civic, and education, then define a contribution your capabilities or institution can support.
