What Is a Fellow?

Identity, affiliation, and contribution

A US Fellow is an individual appointed by the Society to contribute to a public-interest mission through a structured fellowship affiliation.

Fellows may serve through NGO partnerships, research institutes, universities, civic institutions, public-interest organizations, mission-driven projects, or approved fellowship appointments.

“What unites Fellows is not a single profession. What unites them is service.”

The US Fellows Standard
Eligibility

Excellence can come from any field

Applicants may come from science, education, public policy, immigration, entrepreneurship, environmental work, technology, civic innovation, research, humanitarian service, national-capacity fields, or other areas of public consequence.

Demonstrated Talent

Achievement in a field or compelling evidence of potential for excellence.

Public-Benefit Orientation

A sincere commitment to contribute beyond private advancement.

Mission Alignment

A credible connection between a candidate’s capacity and an area of service.

Selection Criteria

Merit joined to seriousness of purpose

  • Excellence in a field or demonstrated potential for excellence
  • Commitment to public benefit
  • Alignment with humanity-scale missions
  • Integrity and seriousness of purpose
  • Ability to contribute to a Host Institution or mission project
  • Evidence of service, leadership, creativity, research, or advocacy
  • Respect for institutional responsibility and public trust
  • Readiness for a defined fellowship contribution
Fellow Responsibilities

The honor carries an obligation

Serve the Mission

Keep the public purpose of the appointment at the center of the work.

Honor the Host

Respect institutional context, supervision, communities, and trust.

Contribute with Rigor

Bring discipline, clarity, and excellence to the defined role.

Strengthen the Society

Share lessons, support peers, and carry the standard forward.

Fellow Benefits

A platform for contribution and belonging

Membership provides a distinguished identity and a community centered on public purpose.

  • National fellowship recognition and appointment identity
  • Mission affiliation and Host Institution connection
  • Cohort and Society participation
  • Public-interest project opportunities
  • Convenings, advisor access, and alumni network
  • Opportunities to contribute to research, reports, and missions

Become a Fellow
Preparing for consideration

Show the connection between record and readiness

A strong candidate does more than list achievements. The application should help reviewers understand how past work demonstrates present capability and why that capability belongs in a specific public-interest context.

Use concrete evidence

Describe decisions, responsibilities, outputs, constraints, collaborators, and results that show the substance of your work.

Explain your direction

Name the questions or communities that matter to you and the kind of contribution you are prepared to undertake now.

Be candid about support

Identify the supervision, access, timeframe, learning, and institutional conditions you would need to contribute responsibly.

Selection in context: Career stage and field shape what credible evidence looks like. Review does not expect every candidate to have the same résumé; it does expect an honest, coherent case for fellowship.
