The Principal standard

Demonstrated authority—not professional potential alone

Principal R&D Scholar is reserved for people who currently hold or previously held accountable leadership on work of U.S. national importance. Qualifying titles may include Principal Investigator, Co-Principal Investigator, Key Personnel, program or technical director, chief scientist or engineer, research director, laboratory or center director, senior technical lead, or another role with comparable authority.

The designation is based on what the candidate was responsible for: the research agenda, funding, team, technical integrity, sponsor relationship, risk, delivery, and transition of outcomes. Seniority, academic rank, years of employment, or a management title alone are insufficient.

Qualifying record

Evidence of leadership across the program lifecycle

Review considers the scale, strategic relevance, sensitivity, funding, duration, institutional context, and measurable consequences of the candidate's work.

Origination

Defined research priorities, technical strategies, mission hypotheses, or new programs grounded in a real sponsor or public need.

Funding and authority

Led major grants, contracts, cooperative agreements, sponsored initiatives, or institutionally authorized programs with accountable responsibility.

Team leadership

Recruited, organized, and developed multidisciplinary researchers, engineers, analysts, program professionals, and partners.

Program control

Established budgets, schedules, work plans, milestones, reviews, quality standards, performance measures, and decision rights.

Mission delivery

Delivered technologies, knowledge, methods, capabilities, policy, or operational outcomes with national or strategic significance.

Translation and partnership

Moved work into operational, commercial, institutional, public-sector, or policy use through credible cross-sector relationships.

Program and funding leadership

Connect scientific opportunity to executable work

Principal Scholars should understand how technically sound ideas become credible, compliant, fundable programs—and how sponsor confidence is maintained after award.

01

Find the mission need

Interpret sponsor priorities, institutional capability, gaps, constraints, and likely users.

02

Form the program

Build the team, technical concept, statement of work, milestones, staffing plan, budget, and measures of success.

03

Secure commitment

Lead proposals, reviews, partner agreements, presentations, negotiations, and required approvals.

04

Deliver and transition

Manage execution, communicate honestly, resolve risk, satisfy deliverables, and move usable results toward adoption.

Technical and mission accountability

Leadership carries obligations that cannot be delegated away

Technical rigor

Ensure methods, evidence, claims, reviews, and quality controls are appropriate and defensible.

Resource stewardship

Protect research funds, institutional resources, schedules, records, and sponsor confidence.

Risk and compliance

Oversee technical, financial, operational, ethical, contractual, security, export-control, and information risks.

Honest communication

Represent progress, uncertainty, limitations, conflicts, failure, and corrective action accurately to teams and stakeholders.

Authority must be real and verifiable. Sensitive or nonpublic achievements may be considered through appropriate verification, but applicants must not place classified, controlled, proprietary, or protected details in the public application.
Team and institutional development

Build the conditions in which others can succeed

Principal Scholars are evaluated by the people, practices, partnerships, and institutional capacity they develop—not only by their own technical output. They should set clear expectations, recognize contributions, resolve conflict responsibly, and create real opportunities for emerging leaders.

Service within US Fellows may include

  • Originating research initiatives and funding strategies
  • Reviewing concepts, proposals, and technical work
  • Mentoring Resident and Trusted Scholars
  • Developing future PIs and Key Personnel
  • Building university, laboratory, agency, industry, and nonprofit partnerships
  • Advising program governance and representing approved initiatives
Evidence and review

Present the record without overstating it

A review dossier may include a CV, public program descriptions, role documentation, award or agreement references, publications, patents, outcomes, sponsor or institutional references, team scope, and evidence of transition or impact. Confidential verification may be arranged separately when appropriate.

Active standing may be maintained through current leadership, proposal and program development, mentorship, technical review, partnership building, governance, or significant strategic service to US Fellows.

Standing can change

Principal Scholars who are no longer actively engaged may move to inactive, alumni, or emeritus standing while retaining accurate recognition of prior accomplishments. Title use must always identify the current standing when material.
