Becoming a Fellow

Recognition, affiliation, and a platform for service

Fellowship offers a distinguished civic identity and access to relationships, institutions, and opportunities organized around meaningful contribution.

Benefits vary with the program and appointment. They do not imply an employment relationship, financial support, academic credit, or a guaranteed appointment unless explicitly established separately.

From intent to practice

What the selection journey asks of you

The process is designed to understand the whole candidate: record, character, field, mission, and the practical contribution they are equipped to make.

Fellowship identity establishes the point of entry; a record of contribution defines what should remain after the immediate work is complete.

Prepare

Gather a clear account of your work, capabilities, service, mission interests, and current readiness. Here, attend to fellowship identity: Recognition grounded in a selective standard of excellence, character, and public purpose.

Apply or be nominated

Submit an expression of interest directly or through someone able to speak to your record and character. Here, attend to mission and host affiliation: A structured connection to relevant work, institutional context, and a defined contribution.

Review and conversation

The Society considers evidence, alignment, references where appropriate, and the conditions for a sound appointment. Here, attend to cohort and society life: Peer exchange, convenings, mentorship, cross-disciplinary relationships, and alumni continuity.

Appointment

Selected Fellows confirm expectations, host or project context, responsibilities, and the Code of Service. Here, attend to a record of contribution: Opportunities to document learning, share field insight, publish, advise, and pursue future missions.

The standard

Principles that hold the work

These tests connect public purpose to responsible practice.

01

A record with substance

Accomplishment may take many forms, but it should demonstrate capability, judgment, and sustained effort.

02

Character under responsibility

Fellows are trusted with institutional relationships, communities, and the public meaning of the Society.

03

Readiness to contribute

Candidates should be able to explain what they can do, where it is relevant, and how they will approach service.

Put this work into practice

Begin with fellowship identity, then define a contribution your capabilities or institution can support.
