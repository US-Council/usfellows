Resident R&D Scholar

A mentored, minimum 20-hour-per-week program for early-career researchers, engineers, scientists, and technologists preparing to contribute in demanding mission-focused institutions.

Program purpose

Bridge academic preparation and mission delivery

Resident Scholars learn how rigorous research is designed, funded, secured, documented, communicated, and delivered in professional environments. The program combines practical contribution with deliberate development; it is not a passive affiliation or an unstructured internship.

Residents work under qualified guidance, learn to connect technical choices to mission needs, and build the habits required for work at government agencies, national laboratories, federally funded research and development centers, defense and intelligence organizations, mission-focused companies, research institutes, and other high-performance institutions.

Who should apply

Readiness is more than a degree

Applicants should bring a credible technical or research foundation and enough weekly capacity to participate consistently. Selection considers both potential and professional conduct.

Relevant foundation

Graduate-level study, equivalent professional preparation, or a demonstrated body of scientific, engineering, analytical, or technical work.

Mission alignment

A serious interest in applying research to public-interest, institutional, national-capacity, or other consequential problems.

Professional readiness

Reliability, ethical judgment, receptiveness to feedback, clear communication, and the ability to contribute constructively within a team.

Direct entry is permitted. Applicants do not need to complete the International R&D Scholar pathway first. International participation does not create entitlement to residency, employment, sponsorship, clearance, or restricted work.
Development curriculum

The foundations of dependable mission R&D

Individual assignments vary, but every Resident development plan should address the following capability areas through instruction, practice, feedback, or documented prior mastery.

Research design and methods

Questions, hypotheses, literature review, methods selection, evidence quality, reproducibility, and defensible interpretation.

Data and validation

Responsible collection, analysis, provenance, quality assurance, limitations, uncertainty, and accurate documentation.

Cybersecurity and information handling

Security awareness, data classification, access discipline, safe systems use, confidentiality, and incident escalation.

AI and emerging-technology literacy

Responsible tool use, verification, bias and uncertainty, human accountability, and protection of restricted information.

Scientific communication

Technical reports, briefings, visual evidence, public-release boundaries, presentations, and communication across disciplines.

Proposals and program formation

Solicitation reading, technical concepts, statements of work, milestones, team roles, deliverables, and compliance awareness.

Project execution

Planning, task ownership, schedules, risk identification, configuration and decision records, reviews, and dependable follow-through.

Cross-functional collaboration

Working with researchers, engineers, program staff, sponsors, institutions, and communities with respect and clarity.

Applied experience

Residents contribute while they develop

Assignments must be appropriate to the Resident's preparation, authorization, and supervision. Learning objectives do not reduce the obligation to produce accurate, useful work.

01

Conduct background research, literature review, requirements analysis, open-source analysis, or evidence mapping.

02

Develop

Support experiments, prototypes, analytical workflows, technical artifacts, documentation, or responsible tool evaluation.

03

Communicate

Prepare reports, briefings, presentations, research notes, review materials, and publication or proposal contributions.

04

Demonstrate

Show technical growth, ethical judgment, task ownership, quality, teamwork, and responsiveness to review.

Mentorship compact

Guidance must be structured and reciprocal

Residents should receive clear objectives, access to an appropriate supervisor or mentor, regular feedback, and a fair opportunity to understand how their contribution is evaluated. Mentorship must remain professional, respectful, and free from exploitation, favoritism, harassment, or coercion.

The Resident is expected to

  • Honor the weekly commitment and communicate conflicts early
  • Prepare for reviews and apply feedback
  • Maintain accurate records and disclose uncertainty
  • Protect information and use tools responsibly
  • Ask for help when work exceeds current expertise or authorization
  • Support a constructive team culture
Completion standard

Documented readiness, not elapsed time

Successful completion reflects consistent participation, assigned work, technical and professional growth, communication, ethical conduct, information handling, teamwork, and readiness for greater independence. The review may consider work products, mentor assessments, presentations, training, and a reflective record of contribution.

Completion may strengthen preparation for mission-focused careers, but US Fellows does not guarantee employment, placement, federal appointment, contract participation, sponsorship, or clearance.

Trusted status has a separate gate

A Resident does not become a Trusted R&D Scholar through program completion alone. Trusted status requires an active U.S. Government clearance and qualifying full-time engagement on work of national importance.
