The purpose

Credibility before endorsement

The goal is not a generic letter, borrowed name, or implied institutional commitment. It is a credible network of people who understand the research question and can decide whether a specific, bounded contribution makes sense.

Begin with a concise thesis or research direction and, whenever possible, support from your advisor or professor at your home institution.

Ask for a defined contribution to a serious research effort—not approval in the abstract.

International Scholar outreach standard
Flexible international participation

Learn and contribute at a responsible pace

International R&D Scholar is a voluntary, part-time, and self-directed pathway for citizen scientists, students, independent researchers, engineers, technologists, and other motivated contributors located around the world. It has no fixed weekly commitment and can accommodate academic, professional, family, and community responsibilities.

Participation may include literature review, open-source research, scientific communication, data validation, technical writing, seminars, citizen-science activity, ethical technology assessment, collaborative discussion, and proposal support. Every assignment remains subject to applicable law, project controls, geography, data protection, export control, and the sensitivity of the work.

A credible network

Build the team deliberately

Not every project needs every role, and no person or organization should be listed without permission. A well-developed network may include:

Home advisor

An international professor or advisor who knows the thesis, degree context, and quality of the work.

Research scientist

A field-aligned scientist who may offer feedback, methods guidance, referral, or a defined proposal role.

U.S. higher-education collaborator

A professor or researcher who can evaluate fit with a laboratory, department, or eligible lead institution.

Industry or mission partner

A qualified technical lead or organization—such as a HUBZone firm—whose capability matches a real opportunity.

Before you write

Prepare one complete outreach package

A recipient should be able to understand the work, the evidence, the opportunity, and the exact request without a long exchange for missing basics.

  1. Thesis or project briefSummary, research question, objectives, methods direction, and intended public impact.
  2. Home-institution contextDegree, institution, advisor, and preferably an attached advisor endorsement or letter of support.
  3. Research recordCV or short bio, relevant publications, portfolio links, and previous work.
  4. Opportunity evidenceSeveral relevant solicitation or opportunity identifiers, each checked for topic, deadline, eligibility, deliverables, and lead-organization rules.
  5. Proposed networkExisting endorsements and collaborators, clearly distinguished from people who have not yet agreed.
  6. One specific askThe exact feedback, introduction, role, or decision you are requesting from this recipient.
Opportunity discovery

Search broadly. Verify narrowly.

Look across federal grants and contracts, small-business research programs, cooperative agreements, fellowships, graduate assistantships, and industry-sponsored research. A search result is only a lead until its official notice is verified.

Grants.govSAM.gov

Check every opportunity

  • Research topic and mission fit
  • Submission deadline and anticipated award schedule
  • Applicant and citizenship eligibility
  • Required deliverables and data rights
  • Eligible lead organization and partner roles
  • Whether SBIR or STTR rules apply

Never describe a person, university, laboratory, company, or US Fellows as committed until authorized representatives have agreed in writing.

Make the request answerable

Ask for one concrete form of help

01

Brief feedback on the thesis, research question, or methodology.

02

A subject-matter-expert, Key Personnel, PI, or Co-PI role for a named opportunity.

03

A referral to a more appropriate researcher, laboratory, program, or eligible lead.

04

Guidance on a specific solicitation, fellowship, assistantship, or sponsored-research route.

05

Permission to cite an endorsement or list an agreed role in a defined proposal or research plan.

06

A bounded meeting with an agenda and the documents needed for a useful decision.

Personalize every message. Explain why the recipient's expertise fits this work. Avoid mass endorsement requests and do not ask someone to infer what you need.

Evidence of participation

Build a record that another researcher can evaluate

A Scholar's record may include completed learning activities, research briefs, literature maps, technical writing, validated datasets, workshop participation, proposal contributions, endorsements, mentor feedback, and evidence of reliable, ethical collaboration.

Active standing may be reviewed according to participation, conduct, contribution, and continued mission alignment. A Scholar may move to inactive or alumni standing when availability changes.

No automatic advancement

International participation does not create employment, compensation, visa sponsorship, graduate admission, funding, clearance, access to restricted work, or entitlement to the Resident or any higher designation. A later application must satisfy that program's independent eligibility gate.

Professional safeguards

Protect people, institutions, and the work

Share responsibly

Do not send classified, export-controlled, proprietary, unpublished, personally sensitive, or otherwise restricted information through public forms or unsolicited email.

Represent permission accurately

Keep a record of endorsements and role approvals. Never imply supervision, employment, funding, proposal participation, lab access, or legal responsibility.

Respect the answer

A scientist or institution may decline, suggest another contact, limit feedback, or request a different channel. A thoughtful refusal is not a program failure.
